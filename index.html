Travel Checklist

The basics for this specific trip. Clothing lists stay separate.

🛂 Documents

  • Passports — carry-on, never checked. Also needed at Sagrada Família (tickets are nominative).
  • Schengen visa — double-check the sticker dates cover 12–20 Jul.
  • eVisa (BRPs are gone) — log into the UKVI account before flying: confirm the eVisa is linked to the passport you're travelling on, screenshot the status page, and generate a share code as backup (valid 90 days). Airlines check status against the passport electronically.
  • EasyJet boarding passes — in the app + screenshots saved offline.
  • All vouchers offline — hotel confirmations, Moventis bus tickets, Tot Montserrat, Klook (Pyrenees), GetYourGuide (Girona). Screenshot each one.
  • Travel insurance — policy doc + emergency number saved in phone.
  • GHIC cards if held — valid in Spain.

✈️ Flight-specific (EasyJet, BFS)

  • Cabin bags — 1 small under-seat bag free per person (max 45×36×20 cm incl. handles/wheels). Large cabin bag or hold luggage only if it's on the booking — check the confirmation.
  • Liquids — assume the 100 ml rule at Belfast International: containers ≤100 ml in one clear resealable bag.
  • Online check-in done for both legs.
  • Power bank in cabin bag — never in the hold.
  • ~02:40 taxi to Grand Central confirmed the evening before — the overnight Airport Express 300 runs hourly on the hour; you need the 03:00 departure (arrives BFS 03:35).
  • Bus tickets: buy the Monthly Return (~£13.50pp — confirm current price), valid for the return leg within a month, so it covers 20 July too. Two singles cost more. Buy on the mLink app or the Grand Central ticket machines. A day return does NOT work — your return is 8 days later.
  • Light snacks + water bottle (empty through security) — no proper meal until Tossa early afternoon.

📱 Phone & money

  • Roaming ON — Settings → Mobile Data → Roaming. Lebara 10GB plan covers Spain at no extra charge.
  • Offline Google Maps — download Barcelona, Tossa de Mar, Montserrat, Girona areas.
  • Apps — FreeNow or Cabify (taxis), official Park Güell app (free audio guide — download + save offline, weak signal on the hill). Earphones.
  • Cash — withdraw at Barcelona Airport after arrival — target €120–150 total for two people. Use a bank-branded ATM such as CaixaBank / Servired rather than Euronet. With Monzo, always choose EUR / without conversion, never GBP. Monzo itself should be fee-free while within your account allowance, but the ATM may show its own fee — cancel and try another machine if it does. Split the cash between you. Use it for small market stalls, Montserrat candles/food stalls, Núria boat hire or small mountain/cove payments, beach toilets/lockers if needed, and tiny cafés/bars. Cards are still the main payment method for hotels, restaurants, taxis/apps, tickets and shops.
  • Two cards, kept separately — one stays in the hotel safe with the passports on beach days.

🧳 Practical

  • Adapters — Spain uses type C/F (two round pins), 230 V.
  • Meds — original packaging, carry-on.
  • Water shoes — Tossa sea entry is rocky in places.
  • Trainers + one light jacket — Montserrat and Núria days. Núria is 15–18°C even in July.
  • Sun kit — high SPF, hats, refillable water bottles. July peak heat 11:00–16:00.
  • Dress code days — shoulders + knees covered for Montserrat basilica; no strapless tops, short shorts, or flip-flops in the monastery. Modest dress inside Barcelona Cathedral too.

Tours

Park Güell

Gaudí's mosaic park · timed entry, book ahead

Gaudí's whimsical hilltop park — the mosaic dragon, the wavy serpentine bench, the terrace with sweeping views over the city to the sea. The famous Monumental Zone is ticketed and timed; the outer forest zone is free. Entry is advance-only (no walk-up tickets). This trip's slot is booked: Tue 14 Jul, 16:30 (entry buffer to 17:00).

Gràcia hills · Metro Lesseps (L3) Daily 9:30–19:30 · last entry ~18:30 4.4 ★ · UNESCO site Basic €18 · +Gaudí House ~€28
Renovation right now: parts of the Plaça de la Natura and the Hypostyle Hall are partially closed for works, and the Vallcarca escalator shortcut is out of service — so use the Lesseps metro entrance or the bus, not Vallcarca. Still well worth visiting; just a few areas affected.
Getting there
  • EasiestBus 24 from Plaça de Catalunya — runs up Passeig de Gràcia (near your hotel) and drops you right by the main entrance on Carrer d'Olot. ~30 min, almost no uphill walking.
  • MetroLesseps (L3, green) then ~20 min walk — flat at first, steep for the last 10 min. Fine, but tiring in the heat.
  • Taxi~€13–16 from the centre. Drops at the Carretera del Carmel side entrance (not the gingerbread-gatehouse one), and you return from the same spot.
  • SkipVallcarca — normally the escalator shortcut, but it's out of service right now, so it's a steep climb. Use Bus 24 or Lesseps instead.
Skip the paid audio guide: the official Park Güell app is free (iOS/Android) and covers the whole Monumental Zone — download it before you go, save it offline (weak signal on the hill), and bring earphones. No need to pay extra for an "audio guide" ticket.
Best slot: book a late-afternoon entry (~4:30–6pm) — cooler than the midday heat, the tour buses have thinned out, and you get golden-hour light over the city. The mosaic dragon is always mobbed; the Pòrtic de la Bugadera (wave-like stone colonnade) is a quieter photo spot.
One-way & no re-entry: the Monumental Zone is one-way — once you exit through the turnstiles you can't go back in. Get your dragon/lizard photo before heading toward the exit.

Barcelona on Foot

Gothic Quarter & El Born

Route 1 of the Barcelona on Foot series
2.5 km ~2 hrs with stops Flat, cobbled Best 9:30–11:30

A flat, shaded loop through the oldest part of the city, starting at the cathedral and ending in the livelier El Born with its tapas bars. Mornings are best: the lanes are quiet, the light slants in, and you beat both the heat and the tour groups. Metro: Jaume I (L4) drops you in the middle of it all.

Open the full walking route in Google Maps →

  1. Barcelona Cathedral

    Pla de la Seu · Plaça Nova

    The Gothic cathedral, not to be confused with Sagrada Família. Stand back in the open square of Plaça Nova to take in the full spired facade. Free to enter outside ticketed visiting hours; modest dress needed inside.

    Mon–Fri 9:30–17:45 4.5 ★ Ticket to go inside (~€11)
    Photo: shoot from the far corner of Plaça Nova — it's the only spot wide enough to fit the whole facade in frame.
    Next: walk 90 seconds south down Carrer del Bisbe — the bridge is right above you.

  2. Pont del Bisbe

    Carrer del Bisbe

    The most photographed spot in the Gothic Quarter — a delicate neo-Gothic bridge arching over the lane between two government buildings. It looks medieval but was actually built in 1928 by a student of Gaudí. Look for the small carved skull with a dagger underneath.

    4.7 ★ · 1,900 reviews Always open · outdoor Free · no ticket
    Photo: stand directly under the arch and shoot straight up. Get here before 10am — by midday there's a queue for this exact shot.
    Next: continue to the end of Carrer del Bisbe — it opens into the square.

  3. Plaça de Sant Jaume

    Plaça de Sant Jaume

    The civic heart of Barcelona for 2,000 years — this was the Roman forum. Today the City Hall and the Palau de la Generalitat (the Catalan government) face each other across the square. If there's a festival on, this is where castellers build their human towers.

    4.6 ★ Open square Free · no ticket
    Next: head east ~8 min along Carrer de la Llibreteria then Carrer de l'Argenteria, which leads straight to the basilica.

  4. Basílica de Santa Maria del Mar

    Plaça de Santa Maria, El Born

    The "cathedral of the sea" — Catalan Gothic at its purest, built in just 55 years by the port workers and merchants of the Ribera district themselves. Inside, slender columns soar into a wide, calm space with very little clutter. Free entry in the mornings; a small fee to climb to the roof terrace, where on a clear day you can see all the way to Sagrada Família.

    Daily 10:00–18:00 4.7 ★ · 40k reviews Free mornings Ticket for roof terrace (~€10)
    Photo: stand at the back centre aisle for the symmetrical column shot. The rose window glows best with late-morning light behind it.

    Passeig del Born

    End the walk on this wide, tree-lined promenade — a medieval jousting ground turned café strip. This is the spot to stop for a mid-morning vermut (vermouth on the rocks, the local ritual) or one of the city's best croissants, and watch El Born wake up around you.

The Ramblas & La Boqueria

Route 2 of the Barcelona on Foot series
Heads up — major roadworks until early 2027. La Rambla is mid-way through a big renovation, so expect construction hoardings, machinery and some removed terraces along the route. It's still fully walkable and every sight below (market, opera, squares, column) stays open — just don't expect the pretty tree-lined stroll from the photos yet.
1.2 km~2 hrs with stopsFlat, downhillBest before 09:30

The city's most famous promenade, walked downhill from the Canaletes fountain to the sea. It's touristy and busy by design — the trick is to go early, dip into the market and the hidden squares just off the main drag, and end at the waterfront. Metro: Catalunya (top) or Drassanes (bottom).

  1. Font de Canaletes

    Top of La Rambla

    The fountain that marks the head of La Rambla. Local legend says anyone who drinks from it will return to Barcelona. It's also where Barça fans gather to celebrate trophies.

    Start here and walk downhill toward the sea — the whole Rambla slopes gently down to the port.

    4.4 ★Free · outdoor
    shoot down the tree-lined Rambla from here for the long perspective with the crowds and kiosks.
    Next: stroll ~4 min down La Rambla; the market entrance is on your right.

  2. Mercat de la Boqueria

    La Rambla 91

    Barcelona's wild, technicolour food market — stalls of jamón, fruit, sweets, and seafood under a stained-glass entrance. It can get mobbed; come early so you can actually move and the vendors have time for you.

    Closed Sundays. On weekdays it's best before 09:30, before the tour groups arrive.

    Mon–Sat 8:00–20:304.5 ★ · 212k reviewsFree to enterClosed Sundays

    La Rambla 51–59

    Barcelona's grand 19th-century opera house, twice rebuilt after fires. The facade is easy to miss in the Rambla bustle — look for the columned entrance. You can peek into the lobby or book a daytime tour.

    Off La Rambla

    A grand arcaded square with palm trees, a central fountain, and lamp-posts designed by a young Gaudí. By day it's calm and good for a coffee; by night it buzzes with bars and music.

    A lovely shaded spot to sit for ten minutes before the final stretch to the water.

    Hidden-gem squareFree · open square

Passeig de Gràcia & Gaudí

Route 3 of the Barcelona on Foot series
1 km~1.5 hrsFlat, wide pavementsMorning light best

A short, flat stroll up Barcelona's grandest boulevard, past the "Block of Discord" where Modernista architects tried to outdo each other, up to Gaudí's La Pedrera. This route is about exteriors — you can admire every facade for free from the street, and pick just one or two to go inside if you want. Metro: Passeig de Gràcia.

Open this route in Google Maps →

  1. Casa Lleó Morera

    Pg. de Gràcia 35

    The first of the three houses on the "Block of Discord" — a stretch where rival architects built wildly different masterpieces side by side. This one, by Domènech i Montaner, drips with floral carvings and stained glass.

    You're looking at exteriors on this walk, so no tickets needed — just crane your neck.

    4.7 ★Free · exterior view
    stand across the street on the central reservation to get the whole ornate facade without traffic in the way.
    Next: walk 1 min north; the next two houses are almost adjacent.

  2. Casa Amatller

    Pg. de Gràcia 41

    The chocolate magnate's house, with a stepped, Flemish-style gable that looks nothing like its neighbours. Quieter and cheaper to enter than Casa Batlló next door, with a little chocolate shop inside.

    Notice how its straight geometric lines play off against Batlló's curves right beside it.

    Daily 10:00–19:004.6 ★Free to view outsideTicket for guided tour (~€20)
    get Amatller's stepped gable and Batlló's wavy roofline in one frame — the contrast is the whole point of the block.
    Next: literally next door.

  3. Casa Batlló

    Pg. de Gràcia 43

    Gaudí's dragon-scale masterpiece and the star of the block — a shimmering facade of broken-tile mosaic, bone-like balconies, and a scaly roof said to be the dragon from the St George legend. Stunning from the street alone.

    If you want to go inside, book a timed ticket online in advance; the queue otherwise eats your morning.

    Daily 8:30–22:304.7 ★ · 210k reviewsFree to admire outsideTicket to go inside (~€35+)
    across the street for the full facade. The tiles glow best in late-morning sun when light hits them head-on.
    Next: a ~7 min walk north up Passeig de Gràcia to La Pedrera at no. 92.

  4. La Pedrera (Casa Milà)

    Pg. de Gràcia 92

    Gaudí's other Passeig de Gràcia building — a wave of grey stone with twisting wrought-iron balconies, nicknamed "the quarry." The rooftop, with its helmeted warrior chimneys, is one of the city's great sights if you go in.

    Closed Monday mornings in some seasons; the rooftop is the reason to buy a ticket here.

    Tue–Sun 9:00–23:004.6 ★ · 111k reviewsFree to view facadeTicket for roof & interior (~€28)
    from the opposite corner to catch the full undulating facade; the stone looks softest in warm afternoon light.

El Raval

Route 4 of the Barcelona on Foot series
1.5 km~1.5 hrsFlat, narrow lanesLively all day

The other half of the old city — grittier, more multicultural and artier than the Gothic Quarter across the Rambla. This is a shorter wander through the bohemian heart of Raval, from the white cube of MACBA to Botero's fat bronze cat. Keep your wits about you in the busier lanes, but it's full of character. Metro: Liceu or Sant Antoni.

  1. MACBA

    Plaça dels Àngels

    The contemporary art museum — a gleaming white Richard Meier building that dropped into the middle of gritty Raval. Even if you skip the art, the big open plaza out front is Barcelona's most famous skateboarding spot, buzzing day and night.

    The contrast of the sleek white museum against the old neighbourhood is the whole character of Raval.

    Mon/Wed–Sun, closed Tue4.3 ★Plaza freeTicket for museum (~€11)

  2. El Gat de Botero

    Rambla del Raval

    A giant, gloriously chubby bronze cat by Colombian artist Fernando Botero, sitting smack in the middle of the palm-lined Rambla del Raval. Locals just call it "el gato." It's become the cheerful mascot of the neighbourhood.

    The Rambla del Raval itself is a wide, leafy strip — a good place to feel the multicultural pulse of the area.

    4.4 ★ · 8k reviewsFree · open 24hOutdoor sculpture
    stand at the cat's head height for the classic shot, or get someone to lean against its enormous side for scale.

An easy, flat seafront walk from the old harbour at Port Vell out to the city beach. Lovely in the early morning before the heat, or in the evening for the sunset and the buzz of the chiringuitos. Mostly about the sea air and the views rather than ticketed sights. Metro: Barceloneta.

  1. Marina Port Vell

    Moll de la Barceloneta

    Start at the old harbour, now a marina full of gleaming yachts, with wide promenades and the Rambla de Mar boardwalk curving across the water. A relaxed, breezy place to begin a seafront walk.

    From here the waterfront opens up toward the beach — all flat, all walkable.

    4.5 ★Free · open promenadeHarbour views
    the boardwalk and yacht masts with the city behind; golden hour over the water is gorgeous here.
    Next: walk ~12 min along the seafront into the Barceloneta district toward the sand.

  2. Barceloneta Beach

    Barceloneta

    The city beach — a long stretch of sand and palm-lined promenade, always alive with swimmers, volleyball, skaters and beach bars (chiringuitos). The iconic sail-shaped W Hotel sits at the far end.

Montjuïc — Half Day on the Hill

A transport-linked half day, not a walking route
Half day (4–5 hrs)Funicular + cable carHilly — let transport do the climbingCastle best late afternoon

Montjuïc is a whole hill of sights spread too far apart to stroll between, so this works as a transport-linked half day rather than a walk. The smart chain: Metro to Espanya for the fountain and MNAC at the bottom, then the funicular up from Paral·lel (covered by a normal metro ticket), then the cable car to the castle at the top. Come down by bus or on foot through the gardens. You don't need to do all five — pick the two or three that appeal and let the funicular and cable car save your legs.

Ticket tip: the funicular is part of the metro network (use a normal T-casual ticket). The cable car and castle are separate paid tickets. The Magic Fountain show is free but only runs Thu–Sat evenings — check before planning your day around it.

Open Montjuïc in Google Maps →

  1. Magic Fountain (Font Màgica)

    Plaça de Carles Buïgas

    The grand fountain at the foot of Montjuïc, framed by the floodlit Palau Nacional behind it. It only performs its choreographed water-light-and-music show on select evenings — currently Thursday to Saturday around 21:00 — so it's the one stop that depends entirely on the day.

    By day it's just a quiet fountain and a good photo of the palace; the magic is strictly an evening thing.

    Shows Thu–Sat ~21:004.6 ★ · 90k reviewsFree showNo show Sun–Wed
    from the steps above the fountain looking down, you get the water jets with the city skyline behind. Get there 20 min early for a front spot.
    Getting there: Metro to Espanya (L1/L3), then a 5-min walk up. This is the bottom of the hill — start here if doing the fountain in the evening.

  2. MNAC (Palau Nacional)

    Palau Nacional, Parc de Montjuïc

    The vast domed palace above the fountain houses the National Art Museum of Catalonia — a world-class Romanesque collection plus Catalan modernist work. Even skipping the art, the terrace out front is one of the best free viewpoints in the city.

    Closed Mondays. The rooftop viewpoint (mirador) has its own small admission separate from the museum.

    Tue–Sat 10:00–20:00, Sun till 15:004.7 ★Terrace views freeTicket for museum (~€12)
    the palace is best shot from the fountain terrace below; for the reverse — the city laid out beneath you — stand on the museum's front steps.
    Getting there: walk up the escalators behind the Magic Fountain (~8 min), or it's a continuation if you came up from Espanya.

  3. Fundació Joan Miró

    Parc de Montjuïc

    A bright, airy museum dedicated to Barcelona's own Joan Miró, with his playful paintings, sculptures and tapestries in a beautiful purpose-built modernist space. A calm, uncrowded counterpoint to the big-ticket sights.

    Closed Mondays. A guided tour helps decode Miró's visual language if you're new to his work.

    Tue–Sun, closed Mon4.5 ★Ticket required (~€14)
    the white geometric architecture and courtyard sculptures are as photogenic as the art — and among the few things you can photograph here.
    Getting there: ~10 min walk uphill from MNAC, or one stop on the Montjuïc tourist bus.

  4. Montjuïc Cable Car (Telefèric)

    Avinguda Miramar 30

    The little gondola that carries you from the middle of the hill up to the castle, with sweeping views over the port and city on the way. It's both transport and an attraction in itself — the ride is the experience.

    Note this is the upper cable car (Telefèric de Montjuïc to the castle), different from the harbour cable car (Transbordador Aeri) that crosses from Barceloneta.

    Daily 10:00–19:004.4 ★ · 27k reviewsReturn ~€17 adultQueues build midday
    shoot out of the gondola window on the way up — the port and the whole city open up beneath you.
    Getting there: reach the lower station (Avinguda Miramar) via the Montjuïc funicular from Paral·lel metro (funicular is covered by a normal metro ticket).

  5. Montjuïc Castle (Castell)

    Carretera de Montjuïc 66

    The old fortress crowning the hill, with ramparts you can walk and the best 360° panorama in Barcelona — port, sea, and city all at once. A weighty history as a military prison sits beneath the gorgeous views.

    Free entry on Sundays from 15:00. The walk around the outer walls is worth it before going in.

    Daily 10:00–18:004.4 ★ · 61k reviewsTicket ~€12Free Sun from 15:00
    walk the ramparts for the harbour panorama; late afternoon light over the sea is the best, and it's the top of the hill so nothing blocks the view.
    Getting there: the cable car drops you right at the entrance. Coming down, you can walk or take the bus back through the park.

La Cascada Monumental

Ciutadella Park · the free "Trevi"

A monumental golden fountain-and-waterfall in Parc de la Ciutadella, built for the 1888 World's Fair — a young Gaudí worked on it as a student. Grand sculptures, cascading water and a reflecting pool, tucked inside a leafy park, and completely free. A calm, photogenic alternative to the big-ticket sights.

Parc de la Ciutadella4.8 ★ · 882 reviewsFree · open parkBest early morning

Mercat dels Encants

One of Europe's oldest flea markets

A sprawling flea market under a dramatic mirrored-canopy roof near Plaça de les Glòries — vintage finds, vinyl, antiques, bric-a-brac and plain junk, all reflected in that famous golden ceiling. Great for a browse and for photos even if you buy nothing. Watch your pockets in the crowds.

Carrer dels Castillejos 158Mon/Wed/Fri/Sat 9:00–20:004.3 ★ · 3,725 reviewsFree entry

Poble Sec & Sant Antoni

Where locals actually eat & drink

A tip worth stealing from the locals: skip the tourist-priced terraces on the main drags and head to Poble Sec or Sant Antoni for dinner and drinks. Poble Sec's Carrer de Blai is a run of cheap pintxos bars (little bites on bread, often €1–2 each); Sant Antoni is buzzy, foodie and relaxed, built around its beautiful market hall.

Grec Festival

Barcelona's big summer arts festival

The city's flagship summer festival of theatre, dance, music and circus, held across dozens of venues each year. The heart of it is the open-air Teatre Grec, a former quarry turned Greek-style amphitheatre on the slopes of Montjuïc.

Shows are individually ticketed and priced per performance; the big names sell out early, so browse the programme and book ahead if something catches your eye.

Films under the stars at the castle

A beloved summer ritual: classic and contemporary films screened outdoors in the moat of Montjuïc Castle, with live music beforehand and a picnic atmosphere. Films play in their original language with Spanish or Catalan subtitles.

You can bring your own picnic or buy food on site. Doors open early evening, music first, film after dark. Book tickets in advance.
